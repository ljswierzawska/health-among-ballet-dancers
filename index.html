Can Ballet Ever Be Healthy?

Beyond swirling tutus and slender figures moving rhythmically to the sound of Tchaikovsky, ballet has developed associations far removed from grace, instead evoking physical pain, eating disorders, and a troubling disregard for mental health.

By Luiza Swierzawska  ·  March 1, 2026

“I don’t know a single professional female dancer who hasn’t had an eating disorder at some point,” said dancer and choreographer Dorota Nowak. “It’s terribly unhealthy to be looking at yourself for hours every day, analyzing every single thing your body does.”

The physical exhaustion and negative body image among ballet dancers are no news to anyone. Yet in an era of constant conversation about wellbeing, has this dialogue reached the ballet classroom, or does it remain rooted in Louis XIV’s century?

Last November, professionals from major American ballet companies told the New York Times that their workplaces are beginning to pay closer attention to mental health, slowly moving away from the culture of relentless endurance.

Even as the dance world is beginning to catch up with contemporary wellness trends — as an example, New York City Ballet partnered with SportStrata to offer performance coaching and sports psychology — research on ballet dancers’ well-being suggests there is still a long way to go.

Part of the problem is how injury-plagued ballet is. A 2024 study published in Sports Medicine‑Open found that nearly all professional ballet dancers reported at least one injury during a season, with many also experiencing illnesses and mental health problems.

The study analyzed 1,627 weekly reports in which 57 professional dancers logged their health problems, including the types of injuries they suffered and the reasons behind them.

Nowak sees some progress. The conversation about dancers' physicality has shifted towards strength training. "There is more discussion about strengthening dancers' bodies so they are more resilient to injuries,"" she said. "That has a positive overall effect on how they are prepared to perform and how long they can sustain their careers.""

Yet the larger problem may be psychological and linked to the negative body image.

According to Nowak, a certain ideal body type still prevails. Even as standards begin to change, the constant focus on physique remains exhausting. “A few years ago, it was pretty normal for ballet companies to ask for your weight,” she said. “Or they would weigh you before rehearsals.”

Fortunately, attitudes are beginning to change and ballet dancers are changing with them. There is growing awareness around nutrition and how young people can stay in shape without sacrificing proper nourishment.

Nowak says dancers are increasingly conscious of their eating habits. “It’s no longer just a cigarette and a coffee,” she laughed. And with therapy becoming more common, it’s something people actually talk about within ballet groups.

"Being a ballet dancer is simply exhausting. People think of it and think it’s so exciting — performing on the stage in front of an audience,” said Nowak. “The question is whether you have support through it all."
